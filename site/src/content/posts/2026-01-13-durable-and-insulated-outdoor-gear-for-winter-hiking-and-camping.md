## Buyer’s guide

Buying winter camping stuff is how I accidentally learn every fabric acronym and still end up cold, damp, or annoyed—so let’s keep this practical. - **What’s the coldest night you’ll realistically sleep through?** Not “maybe someday at -20°F,” but the trip you’ll actually take. Look for temperature ratings (and pad R-value) that match *that*. - **Are you carrying this on your back, or from the car to the campsite?** If it’s backpacking, weight and packed size matter fast. If it’s car camping, prioritize comfort and durability over shaving ounces. - **How patient are you with setup when your fingers are numb?** Some tents/pads/stoves are easy in theory and maddening in wind. If you hate fiddly gear, choose simpler designs even if they’re slightly heavier. - **Do you run cold or sleep like a furnace?** Be honest. Cold sleepers should bias warmer (sleeping bag/quilt + insulated pad). Hot sleepers can save weight and vent more. - **What fails first on you: staying dry, staying warm, or seeing in the dark?** That’s your “spend here” category—shell/jacket, insulation/sleep system, or a solid headlamp. - **Can you maintain it without turning it into a hobby?** Consider drying time, repairability, and how gross it’ll get (and how hard it’ll be to clean). - **What’s the plan if something goes wrong?** Extra layers, a backup light…

## FAQs

**Do I actually need “winter-specific” gear, or can I just layer more?** Layering helps, but winter is where things like insulation, wind protection, and staying dry matter more than “just add another hoodie.” If your sleep system or shelter can’t handle cold ground and moisture, you’ll feel it fast. **How do I pick a sleeping pad for cold weather without learning R-values like a new language?** Look for a pad with a clearly stated R-value and treat it like the boring receipt of warmth: higher usually means warmer. If you sleep cold or camp on snow, prioritize the pad—cold ground steals heat like it’s getting paid. **Is a 2-person tent actually for two people in winter?** Sometimes “2-person” means “two people and a shared sense of optimism.” In winter, bulkier sleeping bags and more gear make space feel tighter, so if you hate playing Tetris at bedtime, consider sizing up. **How many lumens do I need in a headlamp for winter?** Enough to see trail markers, your hands, and the inside of your tent without turning everything into a spotlight interrogation. A headlamp with multiple brightness levels (and a decent low mode) is usually more useful than max brightness you’ll rarely use. **Can I use a canister stove in the cold, or is that a misery hobby?** You can, but cold temps can make canisters sluggish and boil times longer. If winter cooking is in your plans, look for systems known for cold-weather performance and plan for extra fuel (because “just one more boil” is how you run out).

Used gear from reputable shops can be a sweet spot if you’re okay with minor cosmetic scuffs. - **Warm sleeper / mild winters:** If your “winter” is mostly shoulder-season nights, consider a slightly lower insulation pad and a 30°F-ish bag/quilt. Overbuying warmth sounds smart until you’re sweating at 2 a.m. - **Premium comfort over grams:** If you’re car camping or short-hiking in, go thicker/wider on the sleeping pad and consider a roomier tent.
